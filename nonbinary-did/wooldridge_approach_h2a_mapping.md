# Wooldridge-Style Difference-in-Differences and the H-2A AEWR Setting

## Purpose

This note explains the Wooldridge approach to modern difference-in-differences
(DiD), with special attention to the role of two-way Mundlak regressions and the
connection to two-way fixed effects (TWFE). It is written for readers who already
understand standard DiD, event-study regressions, and fixed effects regressions,
but who have not worked much with random effects models or the
Mundlak-Chamberlain device.

The second half maps the approach to the current H-2A project. The current main
specification is implemented in `code/c04_analysis_02_main_dd_event_study.R` and
uses a continuous treatment intensity:

```r
h2a_cert_share_farm_workers_2011_start_year ~
  aewr_cz_p25_l1 * postdummy |
  county_fe + year_fe
```

The treatment intensity is the lagged AEWR bite:

```r
aewr_cz_p25_l1 = aewr_ppi_l1 - wage_p25_l1
```

That is, the real lagged regional AEWR minus the lagged CZ-level 25th percentile
agricultural wage. This object is not a binary treatment indicator. It is a
continuous exposure measure that varies over counties/CZs and years and is
experienced by all observations to some degree.

## Executive Summary

Wooldridge's recent DiD work is best understood as a regression architecture for
modern DiD settings. The core idea is not that TWFE should be abandoned. Rather,
the idea is that TWFE should be written in a way that makes the identifying
comparisons, heterogeneity, pretrend restrictions, and aggregation weights
explicit.

For binary staggered-adoption designs, this means replacing a single TWFE
treatment coefficient with a richly interacted regression that estimates
cohort-by-time treatment effects. For repeated cross sections, nonlinear models,
and nonbinary treatments, the same principle carries through: state the
conditional parallel-trends restriction, parameterize the counterfactual untreated
mean flexibly enough, then estimate interpretable treatment-effect objects.

The Mundlak-Chamberlain part matters because it shows why fixed effects and
appropriately augmented random effects are closely related. A Mundlak regression
adds unit-level averages of time-varying covariates to a random effects model.
Doing so allows unobserved unit effects to be correlated with the covariates. In
linear models, the slope on the within-unit covariate variation matches the fixed
effects slope. A two-way Mundlak regression extends that logic to both unit and
time heterogeneity. Wooldridge uses this equivalence to show that modern DiD can
be implemented with pooled regression while still retaining the logic of fixed
effects identification.

For the H-2A paper, the main payoff is conceptual and diagnostic:

1. The current model estimates one post-2011 change in the slope relating AEWR
   bite to H-2A usage.
2. A Wooldridge-style model would estimate a sequence of year-specific or
   group-specific dose-response effects rather than a single post-period slope.
3. The most important empirical question is what variation identifies the dose
   response: regional AEWR changes, local CZ wage changes, or residual variation
   from their difference.
4. Because the bite contains local wage information, a richer DiD specification
   improves transparency but does not by itself solve endogeneity.
5. The cleanest Wooldridge-style robustness check is likely a flexible continuous
   treatment event study using predetermined or baseline bite measures, plus
   diagnostics that show which years, regions, and dose ranges identify the
   results.

## 1. Starting Point: What Standard TWFE DiD Does

The canonical fixed effects DiD regression is:

$$
Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \lambda_t + \beta D_{it} + u_{it}
$$

where:

- $i$ indexes units;
- $t$ indexes time;
- $Y_{it}$ is the outcome;
- $D_{it}$ is treatment;
- $\alpha_i$ are unit fixed effects;
- $\lambda_t$ are time fixed effects.

When treatment is binary and turns on once, the familiar intuition is that
$\alpha_i$ remove permanent differences across units and $\lambda_t$ remove
common shocks. The coefficient $\beta$ is then estimated from within-unit changes
in treatment, net of common time shocks.

In the simplest two-period, two-group case, this is exactly the usual DiD:

$$
\beta =
\left(\bar{Y}_{T,post} - \bar{Y}_{T,pre}\right)
-
\left(\bar{Y}_{C,post} - \bar{Y}_{C,pre}\right)
$$

The trouble starts when we move beyond the canonical setup:

- treatment timing differs across units;
- effects differ by cohort, calendar year, or exposure length;
- treatment intensity is continuous rather than binary;
- everyone is exposed to some degree;
- there is no clean never-treated control group;
- treatment is partly generated by local economic conditions.

In those cases, a single TWFE coefficient can be difficult to interpret. It may
combine many comparisons, including comparisons across already-treated groups,
and it can implicitly apply weights that are not economically meaningful.

Wooldridge's contribution is to show that the regression framework can still be
useful, but it must be written with enough structure to make the relevant
treatment effects explicit.

## 2. Fixed Effects, Random Effects, and Why Mundlak Matters

### 2.1 The Fixed Effects View

Suppose the outcome is generated by:

$$
Y_{it} = X_{it}\beta + a_i + u_{it}
$$

where $a_i$ is an unobserved unit-specific component. In the H-2A setting, $a_i$
could include persistent differences in county crop mix, grower organization,
baseline H-2A familiarity, climate, distance to labor supply networks, or local
political economy.

The fixed effects estimator allows $a_i$ to be arbitrarily correlated with
$X_{it}$. This is why applied researchers like fixed effects. We do not need to
assume that counties with higher AEWR bite are otherwise comparable to counties
with lower AEWR bite. We only need identifying variation within counties over
time after removing common shocks.

The fixed effects transformation subtracts unit averages:

$$
Y_{it} - \bar{Y}_{i}
=
(X_{it} - \bar{X}_{i})\beta
+
(u_{it} - \bar{u}_{i})
$$

The unobserved $a_i$ drops out.

With two-way fixed effects, we also subtract time averages:

$$
\ddot{Y}_{it}
=
\ddot{X}_{it}\beta
+
\ddot{u}_{it}
$$

where the two-way residualized regressor is:

$$
\ddot{X}_{it}
=
X_{it} - \bar{X}_{i} - \bar{X}_{t} + \bar{X}
$$

in a balanced panel. This is the Frisch-Waugh-Lovell view of TWFE: the coefficient
is obtained by regressing the residualized outcome on the residualized regressor.

### 2.2 The Random Effects View

A basic random effects model also writes:

$$
Y_{it} = X_{it}\beta + a_i + u_{it}
$$

But it usually imposes the key assumption:

$$
E(a_i | X_{i1}, X_{i2}, ..., X_{iT}) = 0
$$

In words, the unobserved unit effect is assumed to be uncorrelated with the
entire history of observed covariates. This is strong. In many policy settings,
it is implausible.

For this project, a naive random effects assumption would say that persistent
county-level unobservables are unrelated to the county's AEWR bite history. That
would be hard to defend. Counties with systematically low agricultural wages,
different crop structures, or stronger H-2A employer networks are likely to have
both different bite values and different outcome trajectories.

So why discuss random effects at all? Because Mundlak showed that we can modify
random effects so that it behaves like fixed effects for the slope coefficients
we care about.

### 2.3 The Mundlak Device

Mundlak's idea is simple. If the unobserved unit effect $a_i$ is correlated with
the covariates, model that correlation directly using the unit-level average of
the covariates:

$$
a_i = \bar{X}_i \pi + r_i
$$

where $r_i$ is the remaining unobserved component that is uncorrelated with the
covariate history after conditioning on $\bar{X}_i$.

Substitute this into the outcome equation:

$$
Y_{it} = X_{it}\beta + \bar{X}_i \pi + r_i + u_{it}
$$

This is a correlated random effects model. It is also called a Mundlak model.
The crucial feature is that we include both:

- the time-varying covariate $X_{it}$;
- the unit average $\bar{X}_i$.

The coefficient on $X_{it}$ is then identified from within-unit deviations from
the unit mean, just as in fixed effects. In a linear model, the Mundlak slope on
$X_{it}$ is numerically equivalent to the fixed effects slope under standard
conditions.

The unit mean $\bar{X}_i$ absorbs the between-unit relationship between treatment
exposure and persistent unobservables. The within-unit coefficient $\beta$ is
then not contaminated by the fact that high-exposure units differ permanently
from low-exposure units.

### 2.4 Mundlak vs Chamberlain

The Mundlak specification uses the unit average $\bar{X}_i$. Chamberlain's
correlated random effects formulation is more general. It lets the unobserved
effect depend on the full covariate history:

$$
a_i =
\pi_1 X_{i1} + \pi_2 X_{i2} + ... + \pi_T X_{iT} + r_i
$$

The Mundlak version is a parsimonious restriction of this idea. Instead of
including every time-specific covariate value, it includes the average.

For applied DiD, the important lesson is not that one must estimate a literal
random effects model. The lesson is that fixed effects can be understood as a
regression that controls flexibly for the parts of treatment exposure that are
permanent or common across units. Wooldridge uses this perspective to connect
TWFE to augmented pooled regressions.

### 2.5 Two-Way Mundlak

In two-way fixed effects models, we have both unit effects and time effects:

$$
Y_{it} = X_{it}\beta + a_i + \lambda_t + u_{it}
$$

The two-way Mundlak idea is to model both sources of correlation:

$$
a_i = \bar{X}_{i\cdot}\pi_a + r_i
$$

and

$$
\lambda_t = \bar{X}_{\cdot t}\pi_\lambda + s_t
$$

where:

- $\bar{X}_{i\cdot}$ is the unit-specific average of $X$;
- $\bar{X}_{\cdot t}$ is the time-specific average of $X$.

The resulting pooled regression includes:

$$
Y_{it}
=
X_{it}\beta
+
\bar{X}_{i\cdot}\pi_a
+
\bar{X}_{\cdot t}\pi_\lambda
+
\text{residual components}
$$

In a linear balanced panel, this is closely tied to the TWFE transformation:

$$
X_{it} - \bar{X}_{i\cdot} - \bar{X}_{\cdot t} + \bar{X}_{\cdot\cdot}
$$

This is why Wooldridge emphasizes the equivalence between TWFE and the two-way
Mundlak regression. It provides a way to understand TWFE as an augmented pooled
regression rather than as a mysterious black-box demeaning operation.

That matters for DiD because it lets us add the interactions needed for
heterogeneous effects while still keeping the familiar regression machinery.

## 3. Wooldridge's DiD Logic

### 3.1 The Problem With a Single TWFE Coefficient

In modern DiD, treatment effects often vary:

- across treated cohorts;
- across event time;
- across calendar time;
- across treatment intensity;
- across baseline characteristics.

If we estimate:

$$
Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \lambda_t + \beta D_{it} + u_{it}
$$

then $\beta$ is forced to summarize all of that heterogeneity. In some designs,
the resulting weighted average can be difficult to interpret.

Wooldridge's solution is to write a more saturated regression. In a binary
staggered-adoption setting, the target object is often a set of group-time
average treatment effects:

$$
ATT(g,t)
$$

where $g$ is the first treatment period and $t$ is calendar time. A flexible
regression can estimate these directly by interacting cohort indicators with
time indicators.

### 3.2 Extended TWFE

The extended TWFE, or ETWFE, idea is:

$$
Y_{it}
=
\alpha_i
+
\lambda_t
+
\sum_g \sum_t \theta_{gt}
  1\{G_i = g\} 1\{t\}
+
u_{it}
$$

with appropriate omitted categories and sample restrictions. The coefficients
$\theta_{gt}$ recover interpretable cohort-time treatment effects under
conditional parallel trends.

The key difference from ordinary TWFE is that ETWFE does not force one common
treatment coefficient. It lets treatment effects differ by cohort and time.

### 3.3 Conditional Parallel Trends

The identifying assumption is not simply "parallel trends" in the abstract. It
is a restriction on untreated potential outcomes. A generic version is:

$$
E(Y_{it}(0) - Y_{is}(0) | G_i = g, X_i)
=
E(Y_{it}(0) - Y_{is}(0) | G_i = \text{comparison}, X_i)
$$

for relevant periods $s$ and $t$.

In regression terms, this means we must specify the untreated outcome evolution
richly enough. Wooldridge's framework is attractive because it makes clear what
is being conditioned on and how heterogeneity is handled.

### 3.4 Event Studies as Treatment-Effect Paths

The event-study version replaces a single post-treatment coefficient with
relative-time coefficients:

$$
Y_{it}
=
\alpha_i
+
\lambda_t
+
\sum_{k \ne -1} \theta_k 1\{t - G_i = k\}
+
u_{it}
$$

In Wooldridge-style implementations, the event-study coefficients should be
constructed from well-defined group-time effects or from a regression that
properly accounts for treatment-effect heterogeneity.

The pre-period coefficients are not proof of identification, but they are
diagnostic. If high-exposure or early-treated units were already trending
differently before treatment, the parallel-trends story is weaker.

### 3.5 Nonbinary and Continuous Treatments

The H-2A setting is not a binary treatment design. There is no clean "treated"
versus "untreated" split. Instead, every county faces some AEWR environment, and
the relevant treatment is intensity:

$$
Bite_{it} = AEWR_{r(i),t} - w^{25}_{cz(i),t}
$$

In a continuous-treatment DiD, the target is usually a dose-response object:

$$
\frac{\partial E[Y_{it}(d) | \cdot]}{\partial d}
$$

or an average contrast between higher and lower treatment intensities:

$$
E[Y_{it}(d_H) - Y_{it}(d_L) | \cdot]
$$

The identifying assumption is a continuous-dose version of parallel trends. A
simple version says that, absent the policy exposure, changes in outcomes would
not have been systematically related to the treatment dose after conditioning on
the chosen controls and fixed effects.

This is stronger than it may first appear. If the dose is partly determined by
local wages, then treatment intensity may be correlated with local labor-market
shocks that also affect H-2A demand.

## 4. Current H-2A Specification

### 4.1 Outcome

The main outcome is:

```r
h2a_cert_share_farm_workers_2011_start_year
```

This is the number of H-2A workers certified in the start year, normalized by
2011 farm employment in the county. The normalization fixes the denominator at a
pre-period baseline, which avoids mechanically changing the outcome because farm
employment changes over time.

Let:

$$
Y_{ct}
=
\frac{\text{H-2A workers certified}_{ct}}
       {\text{farm employment}_{c,2011}}
$$

where $c$ indexes counties and $t$ indexes years.

### 4.2 Treatment Intensity

The main treatment variable is:

```r
aewr_cz_p25_l1 = aewr_ppi_l1 - wage_p25_l1
```

In notation:

$$
Bite_{ct-1}
=
AEWR_{r(c),t-1}^{real}
-
w^{25}_{cz(c),t-1}
$$

where:

- $AEWR_{r(c),t-1}^{real}$ is the lagged real AEWR in county $c$'s AEWR region;
- $w^{25}_{cz(c),t-1}$ is the lagged 25th percentile agricultural wage in the
  county's commuting zone.

The treatment intensity is higher when the regional AEWR is high relative to the
local low-wage agricultural labor market.

This variable has two conceptually distinct components:

$$
Bite_{ct-1}
=
\underbrace{AEWR_{r(c),t-1}^{real}}_{\text{regional policy wage}}
-
\underbrace{w^{25}_{cz(c),t-1}}_{\text{local wage distribution}}
$$

This decomposition is central. The first component is set at the AEWR-region
level. The second component is local and may reflect precisely the labor-market
conditions that also affect H-2A usage.

### 4.3 Main Regression

The main regression is:

$$
Y_{ct}
=
\alpha_c
+
\lambda_t
+
\beta_0 Bite_{ct-1}
+
\beta_1 (Bite_{ct-1} \times Post_t)
+
u_{ct}
$$

with optional controls:

$$
+ X_{ct}'\gamma
$$

where:

- $\alpha_c$ are county fixed effects;
- $\lambda_t$ are year fixed effects;
- $Post_t = 1$ after 2011;
- $X_{ct}$ includes log population and the employment-to-population ratio in
  controlled specifications.

Because $Post_t$ is collinear with year fixed effects, the model does not
separately estimate a post dummy. The coefficient of interest is $\beta_1$.

### 4.4 Interpretation of the Current Coefficient

The coefficient $\beta_1$ is a change in slope. It asks:

> After 2011, did the relationship between lagged AEWR bite and H-2A usage
> become more positive or more negative, relative to the pre-2012 relationship,
> after removing county fixed effects and common year shocks?

It is not a simple treatment-control DiD. It is a continuous-dose DiD with a
single post-period slope change.

The current event-study specification replaces $Post_t$ with year indicators:

$$
Y_{ct}
=
\alpha_c
+
\lambda_t
+
\sum_{\tau \ne 2011}
\beta_\tau
(Bite_{ct-1} \times 1\{t=\tau\})
+
u_{ct}
$$

The omitted year is 2011. Each $\beta_\tau$ is the slope relating bite to H-2A
usage in year $\tau$, relative to the slope in 2011.

This is closer to a Wooldridge-style event study because it does not collapse
all post years into one coefficient. But it is still restrictive because it
assumes a linear dose response and uses the time-varying bite itself as the dose.

## 5. Mapping Wooldridge to the H-2A Setting

### 5.1 What Are the "Groups"?

In canonical staggered DiD, the group is often the treatment cohort. In the H-2A
setting, there is no treatment cohort because treatment does not switch from zero
to one. We need to define groups in a way that makes sense for continuous
exposure.

Possible group definitions include:

1. Baseline bite groups:

   $$
   G_c = \text{quartile of } Bite_{c,2011}
   $$

2. Pre-period average bite groups:

   $$
   G_c = \text{quartile of }
   \frac{1}{T_0}\sum_{t \le 2011} Bite_{ct}
   $$

3. Baseline predicted bite groups, using only predetermined features:

   $$
   G_c =
   \text{quartile of predicted AEWR exposure from pre-period crop/labor structure}
   $$

4. Continuous baseline dose:

   $$
   D_c^0 = Bite_{c,2011}
   $$

The Wooldridge logic favors groups or doses that are predetermined. That is
because a time-varying local wage component can be endogenous to contemporaneous
labor-market shocks. A baseline bite measure is not automatically exogenous, but
it is less vulnerable to post-period reverse causality.

### 5.2 A Wooldridge-Style Baseline-Dose Event Study

A natural specification is:

$$
Y_{ct}
=
\alpha_c
+
\lambda_t
+
\sum_{\tau \ne 2011}
\theta_\tau
(D_c^0 \times 1\{t=\tau\})
+
u_{ct}
$$

where $D_c^0$ is a predetermined baseline dose, such as 2011 bite or pre-2012
average bite.

This asks whether counties with higher baseline exposure experience different
post-2011 changes in H-2A usage, relative to counties with lower baseline
exposure.

The pre-period coefficients are especially important:

$$
\theta_{2008}, \theta_{2009}, \theta_{2010}
$$

If these are nonzero or trending, then high-bite and low-bite counties were
already on different trajectories before the relevant policy period.

This specification is easier to interpret than the current time-varying bite
event study because the dose is fixed. It asks about differential trends by
baseline exposure rather than continuously updating the dose using potentially
endogenous local wage changes.

### 5.3 A Nonparametric Dose-Group Event Study

To avoid imposing a linear dose response, define bite groups:

```r
samp_base <- samp_base %>%
  group_by(county_fe) %>%
  mutate(bite_2011 = aewr_cz_p25_l1[year == 2011]) %>%
  ungroup() %>%
  mutate(bite_2011_quartile = ntile(bite_2011, 4))
```

Then estimate:

$$
Y_{ct}
=
\alpha_c
+
\lambda_t
+
\sum_{q \ne q_0}\sum_{\tau \ne 2011}
\theta_{q\tau}
1\{G_c=q\}1\{t=\tau\}
+
u_{ct}
$$

In `fixest` style:

```r
feols(
  h2a_cert_share_farm_workers_2011_start_year ~
    i(year, bite_2011_quartile, ref = 2011) |
    county_fe + year_fe,
  data = samp_base,
  vcov = ~cz_aewr_region_fe
)
```

This is often the most transparent descriptive version:

- it shows whether the effect is monotone in baseline bite;
- it avoids assuming linearity;
- it makes common-support problems visible;
- it produces an event-study plot by dose group.

The omitted group should be a substantively meaningful low-bite group, often the
lowest quartile. The omitted year should remain 2011 if that is the base year for
the current analysis.

### 5.4 A Rich Continuous-Dose Wooldridge Specification

If the goal is to preserve the continuous treatment interpretation, the model can
be enriched:

$$
Y_{ct}
=
\alpha_c
+
\lambda_t
+
\sum_{\tau \ne 2011}
\theta_\tau
(D_c^0 \times 1\{t=\tau\})
+
\sum_{\tau \ne 2011}
\eta_\tau
(D_c^0)^2 \times 1\{t=\tau\}
+
u_{ct}
$$

or by using splines:

$$
Y_{ct}
=
\alpha_c
+
\lambda_t
+
\sum_{\tau \ne 2011}
f_\tau(D_c^0)
+
u_{ct}
$$

where $f_\tau$ is a flexible function of baseline bite.

This version is closer to the nonbinary-treatment Wooldridge logic. The dose is
not forced into one linear slope. Instead, the dose-response relationship can
vary over time.

### 5.5 Adding Covariate Heterogeneity

Wooldridge's framework allows conditioning on covariates in a structured way.
For this project, important baseline covariates might include:

- baseline farm employment;
- baseline crop acreage;
- crop mix;
- baseline H-2A use;
- predicted H-2A use;
- pre-period farm labor share;
- state minimum wage environment;
- region or crop-type groupings.

The point is not simply to add many controls. The point is to allow untreated
trends to differ by baseline characteristics:

$$
Y_{ct}
=
\alpha_c
+
\lambda_t
+
\sum_{\tau \ne 2011}
\theta_\tau
(D_c^0 \times 1\{t=\tau\})
+
\sum_{\tau}
X_c^0 \Gamma_\tau
+
u_{ct}
$$

In `fixest`, this can be implemented using interactions between baseline
covariates and year:

```r
feols(
  Y ~ i(year, baseline_bite, ref = 2011) +
      i(year, baseline_farm_emp, ref = 2011) +
      i(year, baseline_h2a_use, ref = 2011) |
      county_fe + year_fe,
  data = samp_base,
  vcov = ~cz_aewr_region_fe
)
```

This is a key Wooldridge-style move: if high-bite counties differ from low-bite
counties in observable baseline ways, let those baseline differences predict
different time paths. Then the identifying assumption is conditional on those
differences.

### 5.6 Region-by-Year Fixed Effects as a Diagnostic

Because AEWR is set at the region-year level, an important diagnostic is:

$$
Y_{ct}
=
\alpha_c
+
\lambda_{r(c),t}
+
\beta(Bite_{ct-1} \times Post_t)
+
u_{ct}
$$

where $\lambda_{r(c),t}$ are AEWR-region-by-year fixed effects.

This absorbs all region-level AEWR variation in a given year. What remains is
within-region-year variation in the local wage component of the bite.

Interpretation:

- If the coefficient remains similar, the result is mostly driven by local
  wage-distribution variation within AEWR regions.
- If the coefficient disappears, the result is mostly driven by AEWR-region-year
  variation.
- If the coefficient changes sign, the AEWR component and local wage component
  are pushing in different directions.

This is not necessarily a preferred causal specification, because it may remove
the policy variation of interest. But it is a critical source-of-variation
diagnostic.

## 6. How Mundlak Helps Interpret the H-2A Regressions

### 6.1 Current TWFE as Residualized Regression

The current main coefficient can be understood through Frisch-Waugh-Lovell.
Define:

$$
D_{ct} = Bite_{ct-1} \times Post_t
$$

Because the regression also includes $Bite_{ct-1}$, county fixed effects, and
year fixed effects, the identifying variation in $D_{ct}$ is:

$$
\tilde{D}_{ct}
=
D_{ct}
-
\widehat{E}(D_{ct} | Bite_{ct-1}, \alpha_c, \lambda_t)
$$

The coefficient is:

$$
\hat{\beta}_1
=
\frac{\sum_{ct}\tilde{D}_{ct}\tilde{Y}_{ct}}
       {\sum_{ct}\tilde{D}_{ct}^2}
$$

where $\tilde{Y}_{ct}$ is the outcome residualized on the same controls except
for $D_{ct}$.

This is the object whose distribution should be studied. Raw ECDFs of the bite
are useful, but ECDFs of the residualized identifying regressor are more directly
connected to the estimate.

### 6.2 The Mundlak Interpretation

A two-way Mundlak version of the same logic would include the relevant unit and
time averages of the treatment variables rather than absorbing fixed effects
directly. For a simplified treatment variable $Bite_{ct-1}$, the augmented pooled
regression would include:

$$
Bite_{ct-1}
$$

plus county-level means:

$$
\bar{Bite}_{c\cdot}
$$

and year-level means:

$$
\bar{Bite}_{\cdot t}
$$

The coefficient on the time-varying bite is then interpreted as a within-county,
net-of-year relationship, not as a cross-sectional comparison between high-bite
and low-bite counties.

For the H-2A paper, this helps communicate the design:

> We are not comparing permanently high-bite counties to permanently low-bite
> counties. County fixed effects, or equivalently the Mundlak county averages,
> absorb permanent differences in exposure. Year fixed effects, or equivalently
> time averages, absorb common shocks. The identifying variation is the residual
> movement in bite and bite-by-post after those components are removed.

That statement is helpful, but it also exposes the key weakness:

> If the residual movement in bite is driven by local wage shocks that also
> directly affect H-2A demand, the fixed effects/Mundlak structure does not solve
> the endogeneity problem.

### 7.1 Specification A: Current Model, Rewritten as a Dose DiD

Keep the current model, but describe it explicitly:

$$

+
\beta_1 Bite_{ct-1}Post_t

Interpret $\beta_1$ as:

> the post-2011 change in the slope of H-2A usage with respect to lagged AEWR

This should be the baseline, not the final word.

### 7.2 Specification B: Flexible Year-by-Bite Event Study

This is already close to what the code does:

$$
Y_{ct}
=
\alpha_c
+
\lambda_t
+
\sum_{\tau \ne 2011}
\beta_\tau Bite_{ct-1}1\{t=\tau\}
+
u_{ct}
$$

This is useful because it shows when the relationship emerges. In the current
results, the negative coefficients become more pronounced in later years,
especially after the mid-2010s.

The limitation is that $Bite_{ct-1}$ remains time-varying and includes local wage
variation.

### 7.3 Specification C: Baseline-Bite Wooldridge Event Study

Use a predetermined dose:

$$
D_c^0 = Bite_{c,2011}
$$

or:

$$
D_c^0 =
\frac{1}{4}\sum_{t=2008}^{2011} Bite_{ct}
$$

Then estimate:

$$
Y_{ct}
=
\alpha_c
+
\lambda_t
+
\sum_{\tau \ne 2011}
\theta_\tau D_c^0 1\{t=\tau\}
+
u_{ct}
$$

This is probably the cleanest Wooldridge-style continuous-dose event study for
the paper. It defines treatment intensity before the post period and then asks
whether higher-exposure counties diverge afterward.

### 7.4 Specification D: Baseline-Bite Quartile Event Study

Define exposure groups using pre-period bite:

$$
G_c \in \{Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4\}
$$

Estimate:

$$
Y_{ct}
=
\alpha_c
+
\lambda_t
+
\sum_{q=2}^{4}\sum_{\tau \ne 2011}
\theta_{q\tau}1\{G_c=q\}1\{t=\tau\}
+
u_{ct}
$$

This provides the most visually intuitive robustness check:

- low-bite counties are the reference;
- higher-bite groups trace out differential event-study paths;
- pre-period differences are easy to inspect;
- nonlinearities are visible.

This is especially useful if the continuous-dose coefficient is driven by tails
of the bite distribution.

### 7.5 Specification E: Conditional Wooldridge Event Study

Add baseline covariates interacted with year:

$$
Y_{ct}
=
\alpha_c
+
\lambda_t
+
\sum_{\tau \ne 2011}
\theta_\tau D_c^0 1\{t=\tau\}
+
\sum_\tau X_c^0\Gamma_\tau
+
u_{ct}
$$

This is a richer conditional trends model. It says high- and low-bite counties
may differ in baseline characteristics, and those characteristics may predict
different trends. Identification then comes from differences in bite conditional
on those trend predictors.

This is closer to Wooldridge's favored approach than simply adding
contemporaneous controls.

## 8. Source-of-Variation Diagnostics

Before choosing the final Wooldridge-style specification, the paper should show
where the estimate comes from.

### 8.1 Decompose the Bite

Report variation in:

$$
AEWR_{r(c),t-1}^{real}
$$

and:

$$
w^{25}_{cz(c),t-1}
$$

separately.

Useful diagnostics:

1. Raw variance of each component.
2. Within-county, net-of-year variance of each component.
3. Correlation of residualized bite with residualized AEWR.
4. Correlation of residualized bite with residualized local p25 wage.
5. Share of residualized bite variance explained by each component.

If the residualized bite is mostly local-wage variation, the identifying story is
not mainly about AEWR policy variation. If it is mostly regional AEWR variation,
then aggregate inference and region-level shocks become central.

### 8.2 ECDFs and Quantile Plots

ECDFs are useful, but they should be used for several objects:

1. Raw bite by year.
2. Raw bite by baseline bite group.
3. Residualized bite after county and year fixed effects.
4. Residualized interaction term:

   $$
   \widetilde{Bite_{ct-1}Post_t}
   $$

5. Outcome residuals by exposure group.

The current script constructs an ECDF-like plot using the demeaned outcome and
treatment groups. That is a good start, but the more direct Wooldridge diagnostic
is the distribution of the residualized identifying regressor and its covariance
with the residualized outcome.

### 8.3 Leverage and Contribution Diagnostics

For the main coefficient, define the residualized regressor:

```r
D <- samp_base$aewr_cz_p25_l1 * samp_base$postdummy
```

Residualize $D$ on:

- `aewr_cz_p25_l1`;
- county fixed effects;
- year fixed effects.

Then compute:

$$
Leverage_{ct}
=
\frac{\tilde{D}_{ct}^2}{\sum_{c,t}\tilde{D}_{ct}^2}
$$

and:

$$
Contribution_{ct}
=
\frac{\tilde{D}_{ct}\tilde{Y}_{ct}}
       {\sum_{c,t}\tilde{D}_{ct}\tilde{Y}_{ct}}
$$

Aggregate these by:

- year;
- AEWR region;
- CZ;
- baseline bite quartile;
- crop region or crop specialization;
- border versus non-border CZs.

This answers the practical question:

> Which observations are actually driving the coefficient?

### 8.4 Leave-One-Out Sensitivity

At minimum, report:

- leave-one-AEWR-region-out estimates;
- leave-one-year-out estimates;
- leave-one-CZ-region-cluster-out estimates for influential clusters;
- estimates excluding the largest farm-employment CZs within AEWR regions.

The current code already begins this logic by dropping large CZs within AEWR
regions. A Wooldridge-style diagnostic framework would make this systematic and
connect it to the identifying variation.

### 8.5 Support and Overlap

For continuous treatment, overlap means that the comparison units have meaningful
support across the dose distribution. Useful checks:

- pre-period support of bite by post-period exposure group;
- support of baseline covariates by bite quartile;
- whether high-bite counties are concentrated in a few AEWR regions;
- whether the upper tail of bite is concentrated in a few crop systems.

If the highest bite quartile is mostly one or two regions or one crop system,
then a national continuous-dose coefficient may be too broad.

## 9. Inference Implications

The current main regressions cluster at:

```r
cz_aewr_region_fe
```

This gives many clusters and is appropriate for CZ-level serial correlation and
for CZs that cross AEWR region boundaries. But the AEWR component of the
treatment varies at the AEWR-region-year level. This creates a Moulton-style
concern: observations within the same AEWR region and year share part of the
treatment.

For the current bite variable, the appropriate clustering level is not obvious
because the treatment combines:

- regional AEWR variation;
- local CZ wage variation.

Recommended practice:

1. Report the current CZ-by-AEWR-region clustered standard errors.
2. Report state-clustered standard errors.
3. Report AEWR-region-clustered standard errors as a conservative check.
4. For IV specifications using region-year instruments, use AEWR-region
   clustering and wild cluster bootstrap methods.

If the coefficient is sensitive to moving from CZ-region clustering to AEWR
region clustering, the paper should say so directly. That sensitivity is not a
minor technicality; it reveals that aggregate policy variation is part of the
identifying design.

## 10. How the IV Robustness Checks Fit With Wooldridge

The Wooldridge approach and the IV robustness checks answer different questions.

The Wooldridge-style regression answers:

> Can we specify the DiD comparison flexibly enough that the dose-response
> relationship is transparent and credible under conditional parallel trends?

The IV strategies answer:

> Can we isolate a component of AEWR bite that is plausibly unrelated to local
> shocks affecting H-2A usage?

### 10.1 Measurement-Error Revision IV

The FLS preliminary-to-final revision instrument targets administrative
measurement error in the AEWR-setting process:

$$
Z_{rt} =
\hat{W}^{Nov}_{r,t-1}
-
W^{final}_{r,t-1}
$$

This is attractive because it isolates variation in the AEWR that comes from
survey revision noise, not from local agricultural labor demand. Conceptually,
this is a strong complement to Wooldridge-style DiD:

- Wooldridge handles heterogeneity and transparent aggregation.
- The revision IV targets endogeneity in the treatment intensity.

The main risks are weak first-stage power and few-cluster inference.

### 10.2 Agro-Dissimilar Leave-Cluster-Out IV

The agro-dissimilar instrument uses wage movements in other agricultural
subregions inside the same FLS region. It is likely more powerful, but the
exclusion restriction is harder:

- other subregions may share commodity shocks;
- farm labor contractors may operate across subregions;
- migrant labor flows may link the markets;
- weather and input cost shocks may be regionally correlated;
- clustering choices create researcher degrees of freedom.

This design can still be useful, but it should be presented as a sensitivity
analysis with validation exercises rather than as the cleanest identification
strategy.

## 11. Recommended Empirical Roadmap

### Step 1: Keep the Current Model as Baseline

Present:

$$
Y_{ct}
=
\alpha_c
+
\lambda_t
+
\beta_0 Bite_{ct-1}
+
\beta_1 Bite_{ct-1}Post_t
+
u_{ct}
$$

Describe it as a continuous-dose DiD, not as a standard treated-control DiD.

### Step 2: Add the Current Flexible Event Study

Present:

$$
Y_{ct}
=
\alpha_c
+
\lambda_t
+
\sum_{\tau \ne 2011}
\beta_\tau Bite_{ct-1}1\{t=\tau\}
+
u_{ct}
$$

Use it to show pretrends and the timing of post-period divergence.

### Step 3: Add Baseline-Dose Wooldridge Event Studies

Estimate both:

$$
\sum_{\tau \ne 2011}
\theta_\tau D_c^0 1\{t=\tau\}
$$

and the quartile version:

$$
\sum_q \sum_{\tau \ne 2011}
\theta_{q\tau}1\{G_c=q\}1\{t=\tau\}
$$

These should be the central robustness checks for the continuous-treatment DiD.

### Step 4: Add Conditional Trend Controls

Interact baseline covariates with year. Start with a small, defensible set:

- baseline farm employment;
- baseline H-2A use or predicted H-2A use;
- baseline crop acreage;
- baseline crop mix or crop-type shares;
- baseline population or labor-market size.

The goal is not to maximize controls. The goal is to show that the result is not
just high-bite counties following different predictable paths.

### Step 5: Run Source-of-Variation Diagnostics

Report:

- residualized treatment ECDFs;
- leverage by year and AEWR region;
- numerator contribution by year and AEWR region;
- leave-one-region-out estimates;
- AEWR component versus local wage component decomposition.

These diagnostics are essential for a credible Wooldridge-style presentation.

### Step 6: Use IV as a Separate Robustness Design

If the measurement-error revision instrument has power, present it as the
preferred IV robustness check. Use the agro-dissimilar instrument as a secondary
check if the validation exercises support it.

## 12. Suggested Language for the Paper

The current design can be described as follows:

> Our baseline specification is a continuous-treatment difference-in-differences
> design. Rather than comparing treated and untreated counties, we compare
> counties with different intensities of exposure to the AEWR bite, defined as
> the gap between the lagged real AEWR and the lagged local agricultural wage
> distribution. County fixed effects absorb permanent differences across
> counties, and year fixed effects absorb shocks common to all counties. The
> coefficient on the interaction between bite and the post-2011 indicator
> captures the post-period change in the slope of H-2A usage with respect to the
> AEWR bite.

A Wooldridge-style extension can be described as:

> Following Wooldridge's regression-based approach to modern DiD, we also
> estimate flexible dose-response event-study specifications. These models
> replace the single post-period slope with year-specific exposure effects and,
> in some specifications, define exposure using pre-period bite groups. This
> allows us to inspect pretrends, relax the assumption of a constant post-period
> effect, and assess whether the results are driven by a narrow portion of the
> exposure distribution.

The limitation should be stated clearly:

> Because the AEWR bite combines regional AEWR variation with local wage
> variation, the dose may be correlated with local labor-market shocks. The
> Wooldridge-style specifications improve transparency and allow richer
> heterogeneity, but they do not by themselves eliminate this source of
> endogeneity. We therefore complement them with instrumental-variable
> robustness checks that isolate plausibly exogenous components of AEWR
> variation.

## 13. Main Takeaways for the H-2A Project

1. Wooldridge's approach is not a single estimator. It is a way to write DiD
   regressions so that heterogeneity, timing, conditioning variables, and
   aggregation are explicit.

2. Mundlak-Chamberlain is the bridge between fixed effects and augmented pooled
   regression. It shows that fixed effects identification can be understood as
   controlling for unit-level and time-level averages of the treatment variables.

3. For this project, the most useful Wooldridge move is to replace the single
   post-period dose coefficient with flexible year-by-dose and dose-group event
   studies.

4. The key empirical issue is the source of treatment variation. The bite is a
   difference between a regional policy wage and a local wage percentile. A
   credible empirical section should show how much of the result is driven by
   each component.

5. Baseline or pre-period bite is likely a more credible dose variable for the
   Wooldridge-style DiD robustness check than fully time-varying bite, because it
   avoids mechanically updating treatment intensity with post-period local wage
   shocks.

6. Region-by-year fixed effects are a diagnostic, not necessarily the preferred
   model. They reveal whether the result is driven by regional AEWR variation or
   within-region local wage variation.

7. The measurement-error revision IV is conceptually the cleaner IV robustness
   check if it has enough first-stage power. The agro-dissimilar leave-cluster-out
   IV is potentially useful but rests on a more demanding exclusion restriction.
